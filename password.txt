
Password Recovery & Protection Cheatsheet — Legal, Ethical, and Practical Guide
===============================================================================

Purpose
-------
This cheatsheet explains lawful, ethical ways to regain access to locked files and accounts (Excel, Word, PDF, ZIP, RAR, Windows accounts) and describes safe alternatives to illegal or harmful approaches. It does **NOT** provide instructions for bypassing security protections, cracking passwords, or removing protections without authorization.

Important legal & ethical note
------------------------------
- I cannot help with instructions that enable unauthorized access to accounts, files, devices, or services. Bypassing passwords or protections without explicit permission is illegal in many jurisdictions.
- If you legitimately own the data or have explicit authorization, follow vendor-prescribed recovery methods, use official support channels, or work with certified digital-forensics professionals.
- Maintain proof of ownership or authorization (receipts, account control, written permission) when engaging third-party recovery services.

High-level Concepts (non-actionable)
-----------------------------------
1. Types of protection
   - File-level passwords: Office documents (Excel/Word), PDFs may be protected by passwords that encrypt content or restrict editing/printing.
   - Archive passwords: ZIP/RAR archives can be encrypted with passwords.
   - Account passwords: Windows user accounts and system-level credentials protect user sessions and encrypted volumes (e.g., BitLocker).
   - Recovery vs. circumvention: Recovery restores legitimate access (account recovery flows, vendor tools). Circumvention attempts to defeat protections and is potentially illegal.

2. When recovery is appropriate
   - You are the file/account owner.
   - You have been authorized in writing by the owner to recover access.
   - You are performing sanctioned recovery for an organization (e.g., IT admin, with documented approval).
   - If in doubt — stop and obtain legal authorization.

Legitimate recovery options (overview)
--------------------------------------
1. Vendor & product recovery features (first option — least risky)
   - Microsoft Office: If document was protected with an edit password and you are the owner, check for backup copies, Microsoft account-linked autosaves, or Office cloud versions (OneDrive) that may allow restoration.
   - PDF: Many PDF services (Adobe Acrobat/Adobe Document Cloud) provide recovery or password‐reset flows for cloud-stored documents when you control the account.
   - Archives (ZIP/RAR): Look for original sources, uploader, or backups. If you created the archive, check other devices/backups for the unencrypted files or notes containing the password.
   - Windows account: Use Microsoft account password reset if account linked; for local accounts, use official Windows recovery options (password reset disk, admin account, or reinstall/restore) — following Microsoft's documented guidance.

2. Backups & snapshots
   - Always keep verified backups (offline or in separate encrypted storage). Restore from unprotected backup copies if available.
   - Check cloud sync services (OneDrive, Google Drive, Dropbox) for previous versions or copies.

3. Documentation & proof of ownership
   - Prepare receipts, license keys, account emails, or other proof showing ownership or authorization. This is often required by vendor support or third-party recovery services.

4. Official support & certified services
   - Contact vendor support (Microsoft, Adobe, WinRAR, 7-Zip) with proof of ownership for guidance.
   - Use reputable, certified forensic or data-recovery firms for corporate or high-value recovery — ensure a chain of custody and written authorization.
   - If using a commercial recovery product (e.g., Passware or similar), use it in accordance with its license and only on files/accounts you are authorized to access. Follow vendor documentation and support channels.

5. Preventive measures (best practice)
   - Use password managers (e.g., 1Password, Bitwarden, KeePassXC) to avoid forgotten passwords.
   - Maintain an encrypted, secure backup of master passwords or recovery keys (e.g., hardware security modules, encrypted USB with access controls).
   - For Windows, enable account recovery options: link to a Microsoft account, set up a PIN, create a password reset disk, or enable BitLocker recovery key storage in Microsoft account or AD.

-------------------------------------------
- [ ] Search backups: local drives, external drives, NAS, cloud sync services.

Risks, privacy, and security considerations
-------------------------------------------
- Third-party recovery services: Provide only to reputable, accredited firms. Insist on non-disclosure agreements and clear terms about data retention and deletion.
- Chain of custody: For sensitive or legal matters, maintain an auditable chain of custody for media and logs.
- Malware risk: Do not run unknown recovery software from untrusted sources — it can exfiltrate data or install malware.
- False claims: Beware services that promise guaranteed results for any file type without proof of legitimacy.

About commercial recovery tools (high-level)
-------------------------------------------
- Tools like Passware Kit (commercial forensic software) exist and are used by law enforcement, corporations, and legitimate recovery providers. They support many file types and may use CPU/GPU-accelerated methods to attempt recovery when lawful.
- If you own the data, consult the vendor documentation and licensing terms before using such tools. Vendors often require proof of ownership for support and may provide professional services for sensitive cases.
- I will not provide actionable instructions on using these tools to bypass protections; consult vendor manuals or authorized resellers for guidance when you have authorization.

When to involve law enforcement or legal counsel
-----------------------------------------------
- If files/accounts were accessed by an attacker, preserve evidence and contact law enforcement.
- If you are dealing with legal holds, subpoenas, or litigation, coordinate with legal counsel before attempting recovery to avoid destroying evidence.
- For theft or extortion (ransomware), consult incident response professionals and law enforcement — do not pay or attempt improvised bypasses without expert guidance.

Hardening & Prevention (recommended)
------------------------------------
- Use a password manager and unique strong passwords for each account/file.
- Enable multi-factor authentication (MFA) wherever possible (use hardware keys for highest assurance).
- Keep regular, encrypted backups offline and test restores periodically.
- Store critical recovery keys (e.g., BitLocker recovery key) in secure vaults (company AD, Microsoft account, or HSM).
- Train staff on password hygiene and incident reporting procedures.
